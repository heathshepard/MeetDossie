## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### Preamble
"THIS NOTICE IS A DISCLOSURE OF SELLER'S KNOWLEDGE ABOUT GROUNDWATER AND SURFACE WATER RIGHTS ON THE PROPERTY AS OF THE DATE SIGNED BY SELLER AND IS NOT A SUBSTITUTE FOR ANY INSPECTIONS OR WARRANTIES THE BUYER MAY WISH TO OBTAIN. IT IS NOT A WARRANTY OF ANY KIND BY SELLER OR SELLER'S AGENTS."

Key concept: This is a disclosure of KNOWLEDGE, not a warranty of accuracy. Seller is not warranting water yield, well health, or right validity.

### ¶1 — Definitions
Precise statutory definitions with notes.

**A. Groundwater** — water percolating below the surface of the earth. Usually found in subsurface reservoirs or aquifers; accessed by Water Wells.
- NOTE: Unless SEVERED from the surface estate, Groundwater is owned by the surface landowner who may access it by drilling a Water Well in compliance with law + Groundwater District rules.

**B. Groundwater District** — local/regional Groundwater Conservation District, Underground Water Conservation District, Subsidence District, or other special district or authority that regulates the drilling or operation of Water Wells.
- NOTE: Not all land in Texas is subject to a Groundwater District.

**C. Surface Water** — water in lakes, rivers, creeks, streams, and in the bays, estuaries, and arms of the Gulf of Mexico.

**D. Surface Water Rights** — a permit, certified filing, or certificate of adjudication administered by TCEQ that authorizes the diversion, impoundment, or use of Surface Water.
- NOTE: Not all diversions, impoundments, or uses of Surface Water require a Surface Water Right.

**E. Water Well** — an artificial excavation drilled or dug for the purpose of exploring for or producing Groundwater.
- NOTE: Drilling and operation may be regulated in a Groundwater District. Subdividing a property may impact well rights. A Water Well may be owned individually, by groups, cooperatives, associations, or other entities.

1. Identify by permit/filing/certification of adjudication number.
2. If multiple owners of an interest in a Surface Water Right identified in 3A(1), identify each person and respective ownership interest.

**B. Pond, lake, or water tank on the Property (with or without water)?** Yes / No.

### Notices to Buyer and Seller (numbered 1-5 at bottom of page 2)

1. Statutes, rules, regulations, and court rulings concerning Groundwater, Water Wells, Groundwater rights, surface water, and Surface Water Rights are multifaceted and may be complex.
2. Seller may not have complete knowledge or understanding of the Water Rights.
3. Water Rights may be held by OTHERS — consult an attorney who can examine title + water rights issues.
4. Groundwater District rules differ district to district; ad valorem taxing authority varies. Consult local Appraisal District for taxes/tax rates.
5. If parties intend to RESERVE, specifically EXCEPT, or SEPARATELY CONVEY Water Rights — each party should consult an attorney BEFORE signing.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. Is 61-0 required on every Texas transaction?**
A. Not on every transaction, but strongly recommended anywhere water rights matter — rural properties, ranches, farms, properties with wells or ponds, properties in Groundwater Districts, or lakefront/riverfront tracts. For pure suburban resale on public water/sewer with no well, it's typically not needed but still permitted. Check the local practice.

**Q2. What's the difference between owning "groundwater" and owning "groundwater rights"?**
A. In Texas, Groundwater under a surface tract is generally OWNED by the surface landowner UNLESS severed by a prior conveyance (¶1.A NOTE). "Groundwater rights" — the right to produce/use it — may be regulated by a Groundwater District under the "rule of capture" as modified by the district's permitting scheme (§36 Tex. Water Code). Owning the water doesn't automatically mean you can pump it — the district may require a permit and cap production.

**Q3. How do I find out if a property is in a Groundwater District?**
A. Ask the seller (¶2A). If unknown, check the Texas Water Development Board (TWDB) map — twdb.texas.gov — or contact the local county appraisal district. Groundwater Districts publish boundaries publicly.

**Q4. Seller has an abandoned well on the property. Is that a problem?**
A. Potentially. ¶2B(3) asks about capped/covered/plugged/abandoned wells. Under Texas Water Code Ch. 1901 and TDLR rules, abandoned wells must be plugged to prevent contamination. If a well is truly abandoned (not just capped for later use) and NOT properly plugged, Buyer may inherit a compliance obligation. Recommend a Texas licensed water well driller inspection.

**Q5. Does the seller warrant the well's water yield or quality?**
A. NO. The preamble explicitly says this is a disclosure of KNOWLEDGE, "not a substitute for any inspections or warranties." Seller's disclosure of "well in use" doesn't warrant gallons-per-minute or bacteriological potability. Buyer should test water quality and pump-test the well independently. (TREC 61-0 preamble.)

**Q6. What are "surface water rights" and how are they different from just owning a pond?**
A. Owning the pond (real estate) is separate from owning the RIGHT to divert/impound/use SURFACE WATER (permit issued by TCEQ). Under Texas law, surface water in most streams and rivers belongs to the state; use requires a TCEQ permit or a limited exemption. A pond fed only by rainfall on your own tract may not require a right; a pond fed by a diverted stream almost certainly does. (¶3.A + ¶1.D NOTE.)

**Q7. Seller says the groundwater rights were "leased to the neighbor." What does that mean for Buyer?**
A. It means someone ELSE has the right to produce groundwater from the tract for a defined term. Buyer would own the surface but not the current groundwater production right. This significantly affects value — check ¶2.F disclosures. Recommend Buyer's attorney examine the lease before closing.

**Q8. Do I need 61-0 if the property is on city water and has no well?**
A. Practically no — there's nothing meaningful to disclose. But if the property has a POND (even for scenic purposes only), ¶3.B applies and 61-0 becomes relevant. Also useful if the parties want to establish "no water rights claimed" as a written baseline. Consult with the parties.

**Q9. Groundwater District permits — does the transfer to Buyer happen automatically?**
A. Depends on the district. Some districts allow permit transfers with a simple filing; some require re-application. Some cap the permit at previous production. This is district-specific — check with the local Groundwater District BEFORE closing. TREC unverified as universal; district-level practice.

**Q10. Can the parties separately convey water rights via 61-0?**
A. No — 61-0 is a disclosure, not a conveyance instrument. If the parties intend to reserve, except, or separately convey water rights, they need attorney-drafted deed language (per ¶NOTICE 5). 61-0 documents knowledge as of a point in time; it doesn't transfer or reserve anything.
