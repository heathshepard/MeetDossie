## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### ¶1 — Boundary variance
Property adjoins and shares a common boundary with "the tidally influenced submerged lands of the state." The boundary IS SUBJECT TO CHANGE and can be determined accurately only by an on-the-ground survey by a licensed state land surveyor in accordance with the original grant from the sovereign. Owner may GAIN OR LOSE portions of the tract because of changes in the boundary.

### ¶2 — Prior fill disclosure
Seller/transferor/grantor has NO knowledge of any prior fill as it relates to the Property EXCEPT as stated in the fillable blank in ¶2. Seller lists any known prior fill; blank = no known prior fill.

"Fill" here means material (sand, dredge spoil, concrete, etc.) placed on the tract to raise the elevation — often problematic in coastal zones because it may sit on state-owned submerged lands or violate coastal permits.

### ¶3 — State-owned submerged lands prohibition
"State law prohibits the use, encumbrance, construction, or placing of any structure in, on, or over state-owned submerged lands below the applicable tide line, without proper permission." — Buyer's future construction is limited.

### ¶4 — Attorney advisory
Buyer is ADVISED to seek the advice of an attorney or other qualified person as to the legal nature and effect of the facts set forth. Information regarding the location of the applicable tide line may be obtained from the surveying division of the General Land Office in Austin.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. What counts as "tidally influenced submerged lands of the state"?**
A. Lands below the tide line in Texas bays, estuaries, and coastal waters — owned by the state (typically via the Texas General Land Office). The boundary between private uplands and state submerged lands is dynamic — it shifts with tides, storms, erosion, and accretion. Any property whose boundary abuts these lands triggers 33-2. (§33.135 Tex. Nat. Res. Code; GLO administers.)

**Q2. My listing is on Galveston Bay but not the Gulf. Do I attach 33-2?**
A. Yes if the boundary of your property abuts tidally influenced state submerged lands. Bay properties frequently qualify — Galveston Bay is subject to tides. Check the property description: does it end at the shoreline of a bay or estuary? If yes, 33-2 applies.

**Q3. Is this the same as TREC 34-4?**
A. Related but different. 33-2 is for property that BORDERS state tidal submerged lands (bay-front, estuary-front). 34-4 is for property located SEAWARD of the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (i.e., Gulf-front). A property on Galveston Island's Gulf beach may need BOTH — 33-2 (tidal boundary) plus 34-4 (Gulf/beach erosion). A property on the bay side of Galveston Island likely needs 33-2 only.

**Q4. What should Seller write in the ¶2 "prior fill" blank if they don't know?**
A. Nothing. Seller certifies "no knowledge" of prior fill except as stated. Leaving it blank = Seller certifies no known fill. If Seller does know of fill (e.g., "500 CY dredge spoil placed 1998 west of the seawall per permit XYZ"), write it in. If Seller SUSPECTS fill but isn't sure, note the suspicion — best practice: over-disclose rather than under-disclose.

**Q5. What's the risk to Buyer if the boundary moves?**
A. Two categories:
- Erosion — Buyer may lose usable acreage as land is submerged over time.
- Accretion — Buyer may gain acreage as sediment builds up.
Under Texas doctrine of accretion/reliction, gradual boundary movement generally follows the water; sudden movements (avulsion) preserve the original boundary. Litigation-heavy area. Recommend Buyer consult a coastal attorney. (TREC unverified as codified in the form; this is common-law framework.)

**Q6. What if Buyer wants to build a pier or seawall — does 33-2 mention this?**
A. ¶3 warns that state law prohibits construction "in, on, or over state-owned submerged lands below the applicable tide line, without proper permission." Permission comes via GLO structural lease + potentially US Army Corps §10/§404 permits (federal). Buyer needs to plan for a permitting process — not a broker-provided answer, escalate to a coastal attorney or GLO. (TREC 33-2 ¶3.)

**Q7. Where do I find the "applicable tide line"?**
A. ¶4 directs Buyer to the surveying division of the Texas General Land Office in Austin. Practically, a state land surveyor (licensed) can locate the mean high tide line for a specific tract. Cost: variable; not codified. (TREC 33-2 ¶4.)

**Q8. Is 33-2 needed for a canal-front lot in a subdivision where the canals are private?**
A. Usually no. Private canals within a residential subdivision are not "tidally influenced submerged lands of the state" — they're typically dredged from private uplands and remain in private ownership even if tides reach them. But verify with the subdivision plat and title work. If the canal connects to a bay/estuary via a public waterway with a state-owned boundary, ambiguity increases — recommend attorney review.

**Q9. Does 33-2 give Buyer a right to terminate?**
A. No. It's disclosure-only. Buyer's remedy is knowledge before signing, plus the standard TREC 20-18 ¶5 unrestricted-right-to-terminate window during the option period. If Buyer discovers a boundary problem during option, they can terminate under ¶5 (paying only the option fee). After option period expires, 33-2 doesn't create a new termination right.

**Q10. Do I attach 33-2 if the property is a couple hundred feet inland from the bay?**
A. No. The trigger is "adjoins and shares a common boundary with the tidally influenced submerged lands of the state." Inland properties don't share that boundary. Even 100 feet from the shoreline isn't a boundary — the property line is where the deed says it is. If it's not touching state tidal water, no 33-2.
