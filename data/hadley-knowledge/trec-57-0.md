## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### Title advisory
"As required by law, I advise you to have the abstract covering the property known as ______ (Address) examined by an attorney of your own selection OR you should be furnished with or obtain a policy of title insurance."

Texas law (§14.011 Tex. Occ. Code Ch. 1101; also Texas title insurance statute) requires a broker to advise the buyer on title options. The buyer must choose between:
(a) Attorney abstract examination — historical Texas method, now rare; buyer's attorney inspects the abstract of title.
(b) Title insurance — the modern default; an owner's title policy insures against title defects.

The broker is discharging their statutory duty to notify by delivering this form.

### Utility district (MUD) notice
"If the property is situated in a Utility District, Chapter 49 of the Texas Water Code requires you to sign and acknowledge the statutory notice from the seller of the property relating to the tax rate, bonded indebtedness or standby fee of the District."

Cross-reference to TREC 59-0 (or the district's own notice form). If the property is in a Chapter 49 special district, the buyer must ALSO receive that statutory disclosure — separate from 57-0.

### Public improvement district (PID) notice
"If the property is in a public improvement district, the seller must give you written notice as required by §5.014, Property Code."

Cross-reference to TREC 53-0 (Addendum Containing Notice of Obligation to Pay Improvement District Assessment). If the property is in a PID with a levied assessment, that notice is also required.

### Prospective Buyer acknowledgment
"I have received a copy of this NOTICE TO PROSPECTIVE BUYER."
Two prospective buyer signature lines.

### Approval statement
"This form has been approved by the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) for use when a contract of sale has not been promulgated by TREC. The form should be presented before an offer to purchase is signed by the prospective buyer."

Critical scoping language: 57-0 is for transactions OUTSIDE the standard TREC form family. Most residential resale uses TREC 20-18 and 57-0 is UNNECESSARY (title advisory is baked into TREC 20-18 ¶6). Use 57-0 when the property or transaction doesn't fit into a TREC-promulgated contract.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. When do I actually use 57-0?**
A. When the transaction doesn't fit into a promulgated TREC form. Examples: certain commercial deals, unusual property types, transactions where the parties are using an attorney-drafted contract instead of TREC 20-18. If you're using TREC 20-18, ¶6 already handles the title advisory — you don't need 57-0. If you're operating outside the TREC family, 57-0 is your title-advisory vehicle.

**Q2. Does 57-0 replace TREC 20-18?**
A. No. 57-0 is a NOTICE ONLY — not a contract. It doesn't create obligations to buy/sell; it discharges the broker's statutory advisory duty. TREC 20-18 is the contract of sale for residential resale. They serve completely different purposes.

**Q3. What if I forget to deliver 57-0 in a non-TREC-form transaction?**
A. Potential TRELA compliance issue. TRELA §1101.652 imposes duties of loyalty and disclosure; failure to advise on title may trigger administrative discipline. And under Texas title insurance statute, the seller/broker may be liable for uninsured title defects the buyer would have discovered had they gotten a title policy. Best practice: deliver 57-0 (or ensure equivalent advisory is on record) at first substantive contact with the buyer.

**Q4. Do I check both MUD and PID boxes automatically?**
A. There are no checkboxes on 57-0 — the MUD and PID paragraphs are pre-printed statutory recitations. The form always tells the buyer these disclosures MAY apply. Whether they actually apply depends on the property location. Verify with the CAD, the county clerk, and Seller's disclosure.

**Q5. Is 57-0 signed with the Buyer's offer or before?**
A. BEFORE. The form's approval statement says: "The form should be presented BEFORE an offer to purchase is signed by the prospective buyer." Best practice: give it at first substantive contact + get a signed copy back.

**Q6. Does the seller sign 57-0?**
A. No. Only broker + prospective buyer(s). It's a broker-to-buyer advisory. Seller's disclosure obligations for MUD/PID are separately discharged via TREC 59-0 or 53-0 respectively.

**Q7. What if the buyer refuses to sign?**
A. Document that you delivered it (email attachment + read receipt, or delivery timestamp on a signature platform). The advisory duty is to DELIVER, not to obtain signature. Buyer's refusal doesn't relieve broker of the disclosure obligation.

**Q8. Is 57-0 required for a farm-and-ranch transaction?**
A. Not if you're using TREC 25-17 (Farm and Ranch Contract) — that form has its own title advisory framework. 57-0 is for transactions FALLING OUTSIDE the promulgated forms. Farm and ranch has its own promulgated form family.

**Q9. Does 57-0 have to be a separate document or can it be integrated into an attorney-drafted contract?**
A. TREC unverified as to strict requirement. Practical: attorney-drafted contracts often build title advisory language into the contract itself. If your attorney-drafted contract clearly satisfies §14.011 Tex. Occ. Code and equivalent title insurance advisories, you may not need 57-0 as a separate document. Best practice: include it anyway — the belt-and-suspenders cost is zero.

**Q10. Why does 57-0 exist at all if TREC 20-18 covers 95% of residential transactions?**
A. Because the other 5% still need the same statutory advisories. TREC 57-0 (replacing OP-C) gives brokers a standardized way to discharge the title-advisory duty in non-TREC-promulgated transactions. It's a safety net for the atypical.
