## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### ¶A — Definition of "Mineral Estate"
Read the definition carefully — it's broader than most people assume.

**INCLUDES:**
- All oil, gas, and other minerals in and under and that may be produced from the Property
- Any royalty under any existing or future mineral lease covering any part of the Property
- Executive rights (INCLUDING the right to sign a mineral lease covering any part of the Property)
- Implied rights of ingress and egress
- Exploration and development rights
- Production and drilling rights
- Mineral lease payments
- All related rights and benefits

**DOES NOT INCLUDE (i.e., these convey to Buyer with the surface):**
- Water
- Sand
- Gravel
- Limestone
- Building stone
- Caliche
- Surface shale
- Near-surface lignite
- Iron

**BUT DOES INCLUDE:** the reasonable use of these excluded surface materials for mining, drilling, exploring, operating, developing, or removing the oil, gas, and other minerals from the Property. (So the mineral estate owner can consume sand or shale in the course of drilling; they just don't own it as a standalone estate.)

### ¶B — Scope of the Reservation
Subject to ¶C, the Mineral Estate owned by Seller (if any) will be conveyed UNLESS reserved as follows. Check ONE box:
- **(1) Seller reserves ALL of the Mineral Estate owned by Seller.** — Seller keeps 100% of what Seller currently owns.
- **(2) Seller reserves an undivided ___ interest in the Mineral Estate owned by Seller.** — Fill in a percentage or fraction. IMPORTANT NOTE printed on the form: If Seller does not own all of the Mineral Estate, Seller reserves only this percentage or fraction of Seller's interest (NOT of the whole mineral estate). Example: if Seller owns 50% of the minerals and reserves an "undivided 1/2 interest," Seller keeps 25% of the total mineral estate and conveys 25% to Buyer.

### ¶C — Surface Rights Election
Seller **does / does not** waive rights of ingress and egress AND of reasonable use of the Property (including surface materials) that are part of the Mineral Estate for mining, drilling, exploring, operating, developing, or removing the oil, gas, and other minerals.

- "does" → Seller waives the surface rights that would otherwise be part of the Mineral Estate. Buyer is protected from surface disruption BY SELLER; Buyer can enjoy the surface unimpeded by Seller's reserved mineral estate.
- "does not" → Seller retains the surface rights along with the reserved minerals. Seller can enter the property to drill, produce, etc.

**Critical printed NOTE:** Surface rights held by OTHER owners of the Mineral Estate who are NOT parties to this transaction (including existing mineral lessees) will NOT be affected by Seller's election. So if a third-party oil company already has a lease, that company's surface rights remain — Buyer inherits any burden created by prior transactions.

**Default (if ¶C is not completed):** "Seller's failure to complete Section C will be deemed an election to convey all surface rights described herein." — i.e., silence in ¶C is a WAIVER of surface rights by Seller. Practical: leaving ¶C blank favors Buyer.

### ¶D — Existing Mineral Lessee Contact
If Seller does not reserve ALL of Seller's interest in the Mineral Estate (i.e., Buyer will end up with SOME portion of the minerals), Seller shall, **within 7 days after the Effective Date**, provide Buyer with the current contact information of any existing mineral lessee known to Seller. Rationale: Buyer becomes a partial owner of the Mineral Estate and needs to know who's currently leasing/producing on the tract.

### IMPORTANT NOTICE printed on the form
- "The Mineral Estate affects important rights, the full extent of which may be unknown to Seller."
- Full mineral title examination requires an attorney with expertise (mineral title runs SEPARATELY from surface title in Texas; a normal title policy does NOT insure minerals).
- Attempts to reserve certain interests separately from other rights may have unintended consequences.
- CONSULT AN ATTORNEY BEFORE SIGNING. TREC rules prohibit brokers/sales agents from giving legal advice. (This is a TRELA §1101.654 restatement.)

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. My seller says they own "all the minerals." Should I check ¶B(1)?**
A. Only if the seller has verified their mineral title. Texas mineral title runs separately from surface title; a normal owner's title policy does NOT insure minerals. Recommend an attorney or landman title examination before signing ¶B(1). If the seller doesn't actually own 100%, ¶B(1) still legally reserves only what the seller owns ("the Mineral Estate owned by Seller"), so it's not fatal — but the seller's expectation may be wrong. (TREC 44-3 ¶A / IMPORTANT NOTICE.)

**Q2. Buyer really doesn't want the seller stomping around the property drilling wells. How do we handle ¶C?**
A. Check "does waive rights of ingress and egress and of reasonable use of the Property." Seller keeps the mineral royalties/leasing rights but gives up the right to physically enter. IMPORTANT caveat: this does NOT bind third-party mineral owners or existing lessees. If an oil company already has a lease, they can still enter. Recommend a title/leasehold search before closing. (TREC 44-3 ¶C.)

**Q3. Someone told me if we leave ¶C blank, the seller keeps surface rights by default. True?**
A. Opposite. The form's printed rule: "Seller's failure to complete Section C will be deemed an election to convey all surface rights described herein." Blank ¶C = Seller WAIVES surface rights = Buyer wins. Always complete ¶C consciously — don't rely on the default. (TREC 44-3 ¶C.)

**Q4. Seller reserves "an undivided 1/2 interest" and owns only 50% of the minerals. How much does Buyer get?**
A. Seller keeps 25% (half of Seller's 50% ownership), and Buyer gets 25%. The printed NOTE at ¶B(2) makes this explicit — the fraction applies to Seller's interest, NOT to the whole Mineral Estate. This is a common misunderstanding that has generated litigation. (TREC 44-3 ¶B(2) NOTE.)

**Q5. Do I need to attach 44-3 if the seller owns no minerals?**
A. Technically no — nothing is being reserved because Seller has nothing to reserve. But if the parties want confirmation on the record that Seller isn't reserving anything, ¶B(1) or ¶B(2) at 0% could be used. More common: just don't attach 44-3, and leave TREC 20-18 ¶2.E unchecked.

**Q6. Can the buyer drill for oil once they own the surface?**
A. Not if the seller reserves the Mineral Estate — those rights (including drilling / production) belong to the mineral estate owner. If Buyer wants to drill, Buyer needs to own the minerals or lease them from whoever does. Buyer still owns the excluded surface materials (water, sand, gravel, limestone, etc.) under ¶A. (TREC 44-3 ¶A / ¶B.)

**Q7. What's the 7-day contact-info delivery in ¶D for?**
A. If Seller keeps less than 100%, Buyer becomes a co-owner of the minerals. Buyer needs to know who's currently leasing so they can (a) collect any royalties owed to their fractional interest, (b) understand any existing surface burdens, and (c) participate in future lease negotiations. Seller has 7 days from Effective Date. (TREC 44-3 ¶D.)

**Q8. Does the mineral reservation affect the title policy?**
A. Standard Texas owner's title policy insures surface title only; minerals are excluded. TREC 20-18 ¶6.A permits an amendment for "minerals as described in Schedule B" — verify with title company. A separate mineral title opinion may be worth ordering when significant mineral value is at stake. TREC unverified as a rule; industry practice.

**Q9. The parties disagree about a decimal — can we handwrite it into ¶B(2)?**
A. Yes, if both parties initial the change. But for something as critical as a mineral reservation fraction, recommend everyone consult a mineral attorney BEFORE handwriting anything. The printed NOTICE explicitly warns that "attempts to convey or reserve certain interest out of the Mineral Estate separately from other rights and benefits owned by Seller may have unintended consequences." (TREC 44-3 IMPORTANT NOTICE.)

**Q10. Is this addendum required for a normal suburban resale?**
A. No — only if Seller wants to reserve minerals. Most suburban residential sales convey minerals with the surface (or the minerals were previously severed by a prior deed and are unknown/unreachable). 44-3 attaches ONLY when a seller specifically wants to reserve something.
