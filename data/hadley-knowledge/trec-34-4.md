## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### WARNING block (all caps, load-bearing)
- Notice of potential risks of ECONOMIC LOSS to Buyer is REQUIRED BY STATE LAW.
- Buyer must READ THE NOTICE CAREFULLY and NOT SIGN THE CONTRACT until the risks are fully understood.
- By purchasing, Buyer MAY BE ASSUMING ECONOMIC RISKS OVER AND ABOVE INLAND PROPERTY.
- Structures on Gulf-front coastal property MAY COME TO BE LOCATED ON THE PUBLIC BEACH because of coastal erosion and storm events.
- As owner of a structure on the public beach, Buyer COULD BE SUED BY THE STATE OF TEXAS AND ORDERED TO REMOVE THE STRUCTURE.
- Removal costs and any other economic loss from a removal order would be SOLELY BUYER'S RESPONSIBILITY.

### Geographic scope paragraph
The property is located "seaward of the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway to its southernmost point and then seaward of the longitudinal line also known as 97 degrees, 12', 19"" — running southerly to the international boundary from the intersection of the centerline of the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway and the Brownsville Ship Channel.

Translation: any property between the GIWW and the Gulf of Mexico (including Bolivar Peninsula, Galveston Island, Padre Island, Mustang Island, and other Gulf-adjacent tracts).

### Public beach easement paragraph
If the property is in close proximity to a beach fronting the Gulf of Mexico, "the public has acquired a right of use or easement to or over the area of any public beach by prescription, dedication, or presumption, or has retained a right by virtue of continuous right in the public since time immemorial, as recognized in law and custom."

**Landward boundary of the public easement:** The extreme seaward boundary of natural vegetation that spreads continuously inland customarily marks the landward boundary of the public easement. If there is no clearly marked natural vegetation line, the landward boundary is as provided by Sections 61.016 and 61.017 Natural Resources Code.

### Erosion rate paragraph
"Much of the Gulf of Mexico coastline is eroding at rates of more than five feet per year." Erosion rates for all Texas Gulf property subject to the open beaches act are available from the Texas General Land Office.

### State enforcement paragraph
State law prohibits any obstruction, barrier, restraint, or interference with the use of the public easement, INCLUDING placement of structures seaward of the landward boundary of the easement.

**Enforcement:** "OWNERS OF STRUCTURES ERECTED SEAWARD OF THE VEGETATION LINE (OR OTHER APPLICABLE EASEMENT BOUNDARY) OR THAT BECOME SEAWARD OF THE VEGETATION LINE AS A RESULT OF PROCESSES SUCH AS SHORELINE EROSION ARE SUBJECT TO A LAWSUIT BY THE STATE OF TEXAS TO REMOVE THE STRUCTURES."

### Advisory paragraph
Buyer should: (1) determine the rate of shoreline erosion in the vicinity of the real property; and (2) seek the advice of an attorney or other qualified person before executing this contract or instrument of conveyance as to the relevance of these statutes and facts to the value of the property being purchased.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. What's the difference between 33-2 and 34-4?**
A. Very different:
- **33-2** — Property adjoins tidally influenced state submerged lands (bays, estuaries). Boundary variance disclosure.
- **34-4** — Property is seaward of the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (Gulf-adjacent, barrier islands). Public beach easement + open beaches act + state removal risk disclosure.

A Galveston Gulf-front lot may need BOTH. A Corpus Christi bay-front (not Gulf-front) lot needs 33-2 only.

**Q2. My listing is on Padre Island, bay side (Laguna Madre). Attach 34-4?**
A. Yes if the property is seaward of the GIWW. Padre Island IS seaward of the GIWW — the GIWW runs behind (bay side of) the island. Even a bay-side lot on Padre triggers 34-4. Check whether the property is between the GIWW and the international boundary (post-Brownsville Ship Channel intersection, use longitude 97°12'19"). If yes, attach 34-4.

**Q3. What is the "vegetation line" and why does it matter?**
A. It's the seaward-most edge of continuous natural vegetation on the coastal tract. In Texas open-beach jurisprudence, that line marks the landward boundary of the public beach easement. Structures seaward of it (or that BECOME seaward due to erosion) are subject to state removal action. Buyers of Gulf-front homes have literally lost their houses to this doctrine — the "rolling easement" concept. (§§61.016, 61.017 Tex. Nat. Res. Code; TREC 34-4 face.)

**Q4. Severance v. Patterson — didn't that limit the rolling easement doctrine?**
A. The 2010 Texas Supreme Court decision in Severance v. Patterson, 370 S.W.3d 705 (Tex. 2012), held that the public beach easement doesn't automatically "roll" landward following storm-caused avulsive changes to the shoreline — the easement must be re-established. But the doctrine's application to gradual erosion (as opposed to sudden storm events) remains. This is nuanced case law; recommend Buyer consult a coastal attorney. TREC unverified as form-referenced but legally relevant.

**Q5. How do I get current erosion rates?**
A. Texas General Land Office (GLO) publishes erosion rates. The form explicitly directs buyers to the GLO. Also see the GLO's Beach/Dune Rules and coastal erosion planning atlases. (TREC 34-4 face; GLO website: glo.texas.gov.)

**Q6. What if the property is already showing erosion?**
A. Serious risk factor. Buyer should:
1. Get a current survey showing the vegetation line vs. structure locations.
2. Check historical GLO erosion data for the tract.
3. Understand that any structure that ends up seaward of the vegetation line — even years after purchase — can trigger state removal action.
4. Consider whether title insurance (which excludes public easements) is enough protection.
This isn't a form question; it's a due-diligence one. Escalate to attorney.

**Q7. Can Buyer buy insurance against beach removal risk?**
A. Standard homeowner's and title policies do NOT cover state removal from the public beach. Some carriers offer surplus lines coverage; extremely narrow. Ask an insurance broker specializing in coastal risk. TREC unverified; industry practice.

**Q8. Does 34-4 give Buyer a right to terminate?**
A. No. Disclosure-only. Buyer's window to walk is the option period under TREC 20-18 ¶5. After option ends, 34-4 doesn't create a new termination right. Best practice: attach 34-4 to the initial offer so Buyer has full information from the start.

**Q9. What if the property was built decades ago and is still landward of the vegetation line — is 34-4 still required?**
A. Yes. The disclosure applies to ANY sale of property seaward of the GIWW, regardless of current structure position relative to the vegetation line. Vegetation lines move over time — today's landward property could be tomorrow's seaward-of-line property. Statutory disclosure is triggered by property location, not current structure status.

**Q10. Are there federal disclosures that pair with 34-4?**
A. Not directly. But:
- Federal Coastal Zone Management Act — administrative framework
- Flood Insurance (NFIP) — Buyer should pull the flood zone (FEMA CBRS — Coastal Barrier Resources System) — many Gulf-adjacent tracts are in CBRS, which BARS federal flood insurance
- 34-4 is state disclosure only; flood zone and CBRS status should be separately checked
TREC unverified as coordinated with the form; practical guidance.
