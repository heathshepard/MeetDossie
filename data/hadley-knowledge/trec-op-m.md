## Paragraph-by-paragraph rules

### Header — approval statement (unlike mandatory forms)
"APPROVED BY THE TEXAS REAL ESTATE COMMISSION (TREC) 10-10-11 FOR VOLUNTARY USE"

TREC OP-M is APPROVED for voluntary use — meaning agents CAN use it but aren't required to. Contrast: promulgated forms (TREC 20-18, 40-11, etc.) are MANDATORY for the situations they cover.

### ¶A — Additional consideration + item specification
"For an additional sum of $______ and other and good valuable consideration, Seller shall convey to Buyer at closing the following personal property (specify each item carefully, include description, model numbers, serial numbers, location, and other information):"

Free-text description field. Best practice:
- Item type (e.g., "GE Profile side-by-side refrigerator")
- Model number
- Serial number
- Location on property
- Any distinguishing features

The MORE specific, the better — avoids "which fridge did they mean?" disputes at closing.

**Dollar amount separately stated:** The additional sum for personal property is BROKEN OUT from the real property Sales Price. This has tax consequences (personal property may be subject to different taxation) and title-insurance consequences (personal property is NOT insured by an owner's title policy).

### ¶B — Warranty of ownership
"Seller represents and warrants that Seller owns the personal property described in Paragraph A free and clear of all encumbrances."

Seller warrants:
- Ownership
- Free and clear title (no liens, no leases, no security interests)

If a piece of personal property is subject to a UCC-1 lien (rare in residential but not impossible for high-value equipment), Seller cannot use OP-M without first clearing the encumbrance.

### ¶C — No warranty of condition or performance
"Seller does not warrant or guarantee the condition or future performance of the personal property conveyed by this document."

- No warranty of condition (as-is on the personal property).
- No warranty of future performance.

This tracks the TREC 20-18 ¶7.B "as-is" default for the real property — Seller is not standing behind the appliances or personal property's continued operation.

## Deadline math

No day-count deadlines. Delivery of personal property occurs at closing along with the real property conveyance.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. When do I use OP-M vs. adding items to the ¶2.C Accessories checklist?**
A. Use ¶2.C for items ALREADY LISTED there (window unit AC, stove, water softener, mailbox, TV mounts, etc.) — check the applicable boxes. Use OP-M for items NOT listed in ¶2.C (refrigerator, washer, dryer, freestanding safe, patio furniture, riding mower, etc.). OP-M gets attached to the contract as a separate addendum.

**Q2. Why is the personal property sum separately stated?**
A. Because personal property has different treatment:
- Not covered by owner's title insurance.
- May affect the appraisal (some appraisers exclude personal property).
- Could have sales tax implications in some scenarios (rare in real estate context, TREC unverified).
- Lender may not want to finance personal property (they lend against real property collateral).
Separating the sum lets everyone properly categorize what's being sold. TREC unverified as a codified reason but industry practice.

**Q3. Does the buyer's lender include the personal property amount in the loan?**
A. Usually NO — lenders finance real property, not personal property. This is a common issue: Buyer includes $5,000 for a refrigerator in the sales price, but the lender only lends against the real property value. Best practice: keep the personal property sum modest, allocate it cleanly in OP-M, and confirm with the lender in advance.

**Q4. Seller says the fridge conveys — do we need OP-M?**
A. Recommended. TREC 20-18 ¶2.C doesn't list refrigerator by default. Without OP-M (or a written amendment via TREC 39-9), the refrigerator is technically NOT part of the conveyance. At closing, Seller could legally take it. Belt-and-suspenders: attach OP-M with the refrigerator specifically identified, or check-mark refrigerator in ¶2.C if a customized version of the form allows it.

**Q5. What if the fridge breaks between contract and closing?**
A. Per ¶C, Seller doesn't warrant condition or future performance. If it breaks, Buyer's only remedies come from TREC 20-18 ¶14 (Casualty Loss) — but ¶14 covers real property casualty. Personal property casualty isn't specifically addressed. Practical: if a listed appliance dies, negotiate a credit at closing via TREC 39-9. TREC unverified as a codified rule.

**Q6. Can we describe items generically ("all kitchen appliances")?**
A. Not recommended. ¶A directs "specify each item carefully, include description, model numbers, serial numbers, location, and other information." Generic "all appliances" or "everything shown" is a fight-magnet at closing. Take 10 minutes and list each item precisely.

**Q7. Are there items OP-M should NOT be used for?**
A. Yes. OP-M is for PERSONAL property (chattels). It's not appropriate for:
- Real property fixtures (they convey with the deed anyway; don't need OP-M).
- Items already listed in TREC 20-18 ¶2.C (use the ¶2.C checkboxes instead).
- Items subject to a lease or lien Seller can't clear (e.g., leased solar equipment — needs a lease-transfer via TREC 20-18 ¶4).
- Vehicles / mobile homes that require separate title transfer (DMV process; not TREC).

**Q8. Does OP-M convey pool equipment, hot tub, water softener?**
A. Depends. Pool equipment and permanently-plumbed water softeners often qualify as FIXTURES (they convey automatically with the real property under Texas fixture law). A freestanding hot tub or portable water softener is PERSONAL property — use ¶2.C if it's listed, or OP-M if not. Ask: is it plumbed/permanently installed vs. plug-in-and-carry-away? Plumbed = likely fixture; portable = personal property.

**Q9. Seller wants to sell the tractor for an extra $3,000. Should I use OP-M?**
A. Yes — that's exactly OP-M's use case. But be careful: a tractor may have a TITLE that requires separate transfer via county tax office. OP-M alone doesn't transfer titled property. Include the tractor's VIN in the ¶A description, and coordinate the title transfer with the closing.

**Q10. Buyer asked me to add a "sentimental item" (grandma's rocking chair). Weird but should I use OP-M?**
A. Yes if Seller agrees. OP-M is broad enough to cover any personal property Seller is willing to include. Describe: "1 antique wooden rocking chair currently located in the master bedroom, cane seat, painted white, approx. 4 feet tall." No dollar allocation needed if the item is being conveyed as part of the overall consideration (fill ¶A with $0 or a nominal $1 if TREC pattern requires a positive number).
