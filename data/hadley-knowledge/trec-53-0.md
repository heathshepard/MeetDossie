### Notice body (statutory recitation)
Buyer is obligated to pay assessments to the levying municipality or county for "Authorized Improvements" undertaken for the benefit of the Property within the District.

**KEY WARNING (all caps in the form):**
"AN ASSESSMENT HAS BEEN LEVIED AGAINST YOUR PROPERTY FOR THE AUTHORIZED IMPROVEMENTS, WHICH MAY BE PAID IN FULL AT ANY TIME. IF THE ASSESSMENT IS NOT PAID IN FULL, IT WILL BE DUE AND PAYABLE IN ANNUAL INSTALLMENTS THAT WILL VARY FROM YEAR TO YEAR DEPENDING ON THE AMOUNT OF INTEREST PAID, COLLECTION COSTS, ADMINISTRATIVE COSTS, AND DELINQUENCY COSTS."

- Buyer may prepay assessment in full at any time (no lockout).
- If not prepaid, annual installments are variable — year-to-year swings driven by interest, collection, administrative, delinquency costs.

### Contact / lookup fields (fillable)
- **Where to get the exact assessment amount** — insert name of municipality or county.
- **Who approves annual installments** — insert name of city council or county commissioners court. Approved each year in the annual service plan update for the district.
- **More info about assessments (amounts + due dates)** — insert name of municipality or county.

### Consequences of nonpayment
"Your failure to pay any assessment or any annual installment may result in penalties and interest being added to what you owe or in a lien on and the foreclosure of your property."

### Buyer acknowledgment
"The undersigned purchaser acknowledges receipt of this notice BEFORE the effective date of a binding contract for the purchase of the real property at the address described above."

## Deadline math

| Event | Clock | Trigger | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buyer acknowledges receipt | BEFORE effective date of binding contract | Property is in a PID with a levied assessment | Statutory disclosure satisfied |
| Buyer prepays assessment in full | Any time | Buyer's election | Assessment obligation terminated |
| Annual installment due | Set annually by governing body | Not prepaid | Variable payment |
| Nonpayment consequences | Immediate | Late/missed installment | Penalties + interest + potential foreclosure |

**Critical timing:** Unlike TREC 59-0 (which allows receipt at OR BEFORE binding contract OR at closing), TREC 53-0 requires acknowledgment BEFORE the effective date. Delivering after execution is not compliant. This tracks Tex. Prop. Code §5.014.

## Common Q&A a working TX agent would ask

**Q1. How is a PID different from a MUD or an HOA?**
A. Three separate concepts:
- **PID (Public Improvement District)** — created by municipality/county under Local Government Code Ch. 372/382; levies ASSESSMENTS (not taxes) on properties benefiting from specific improvements (streets, sidewalks, landscaping). TREC 53-0 covers this.
- **MUD (Municipal Utility District)** — political subdivision under Texas Water Code Ch. 49; levies AD VALOREM TAXES for water/sewer/drainage. TREC 59-0 covers this.
- **HOA (Homeowners Association)** — private, contract-based dues via CC&Rs; NOT a taxing entity. TREC 36-11 covers this.
The three can co-exist on one property. Attach each applicable disclosure.

**Q2. What if the property is in a PID but no assessment has been levied yet?**
A. TREC 53-0 is triggered when an assessment IS levied. If no assessment yet, the property is in the "district" but there's no obligation to pay — however, the developer likely intends a future levy. Recommend Buyer contact the district for status. If levy is imminent, disclose in special provisions or ¶11 (via broker's-lawyer input). TREC unverified whether 53-0 is technically required pre-levy.

**Q3. What's the difference between Chapter 372 Subchapter A and Chapter 382?**
A. Both create Public Improvement Districts, but differ in structure:
- **Chapter 372 Subchapter A** — most common PID form; municipality or county creates the district by petition of landowners; ISD-style boundaries.
- **Chapter 382** — Municipal Management Districts and certain specialized PIDs; often used for commercial/mixed-use areas; broader powers including limited taxing authority.
Check the ONE box that matches the enabling statute. Ask the developer or municipal PID coordinator if unclear.

**Q4. Can the buyer terminate if the PID assessment turns out higher than expected?**
A. Not under TREC 53-0 — it's disclosure only. Buyer's remedy is (a) exercise the TREC 20-18 ¶5 option-period termination if they discover the burden during the unrestricted-right window, or (b) reject the deal before signing. Best practice: attach 53-0 with the actual assessment amount BEFORE offer, so Buyer prices it in.

**Q5. What's the foreclosure risk from nonpayment?**
A. Under Chapter 372, PID assessments create a FIRST LIEN on the property — often superior to a mortgage. Nonpayment can trigger foreclosure by the levying authority. Buyer should treat PID installments as seriously as property taxes. (TREC 53-0 face + Local Gov't Code §372.018.)

**Q6. Do I need to attach both 53-0 and 59-0 if the property is in a PID AND a MUD?**
A. Yes. Different districts, different statutes, different disclosure forms. 53-0 for PID; 59-0 for MUD (or other Chapter 49 Water Code special district). Attach both.

**Q7. Who fills in the "exact amount" fields?**
A. Seller (with agent's help). Seller obtains the assessment amount from the municipality/county PID coordinator. If exact amount is unknown at contract execution, note "TBD — contact [district contact] for current amount" — but recognize this may make the disclosure incomplete. Best practice: pull the current amount from the district BEFORE closing.

**Q8. What happens if Seller doesn't provide 53-0 before Effective Date?**
A. Tex. Prop. Code §5.014 gives Buyer a right to terminate the contract WITHOUT PENALTY if the required PID notice is not delivered prior to executing the contract. Buyer can also seek statutory damages. This is one of the higher-stakes disclosures — DO NOT delay. (Tex. Prop. Code §5.014 — TREC unverified as to exact statutory-damages amount without checking current text.)

**Q9. Is 53-0 required for a re-sale of a home already in the PID?**
A. Yes. §5.014 applies to every subsequent conveyance, not just the initial sale from the developer. The obligation runs with the land (it's an assessment lien), so every buyer needs the disclosure.

**Q10. Can the parties waive 53-0?**
A. No. It's a statutory notice under Tex. Prop. Code §5.014. Buyer's waiver is not effective against the statute. Attach the form even if Buyer claims to already know about the PID.
